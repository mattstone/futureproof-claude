## 1. EPM and the Estate — How It Works

When an EPM consumer dies, the estate settlement follows this sequence:

```
Consumer dies
     ↓
Property valued (independent valuation)
     ↓
EPM mortgage balance calculated (principal + accrued margin)
     ↓
Investment portfolio valued (current market value)
     ↓
NNEG check: Does mortgage balance exceed property value?
     ├── NO  → Estate repays mortgage from property sale, keeps surplus
     └── YES → Lender absorbs loss (NNEG guarantee), estate owes nothing beyond property value
     ↓
Portfolio returned to estate (Model B: liquidated, proceeds to estate)
     OR
Portfolio retained by lender (Model B alt: lender keeps portfolio, mortgage written off)
     ↓
Net estate distributed to beneficiaries
```

### 1.1 What Beneficiaries Inherit

| Asset | Without EPM | With EPM (Model B) |
|-------|-------------|---------------------|
| Property equity | 100% of property value | Property value minus EPM mortgage balance |
| Investment portfolio | N/A | Portfolio value (if returned to estate) or $0 (if lender retains) |
| Other assets | Unchanged | Unchanged |
| **Consumer enjoyed during lifetime** | **$0 from home equity** | **$500K-$800K+ tax-free income** |

### 1.2 The Key Consumer Message

> "Your home equity is currently frozen — it benefits no one until you sell or die. EPM unlocks that equity as tax-free income during your lifetime. Your beneficiaries still inherit the property (less the mortgage) plus the investment portfolio. And the NNEG guarantee means they can never owe more than the property is worth."

## 2. Australia — No Death Tax, But CGT Applies

### 2.1 Estate Settlement

Australia has **no inheritance tax, estate tax, or death duties** (abolished 1979). However, CGT applies to inherited assets in certain circumstances.

| Event | Tax Treatment |
|-------|---------------|
| Property passes to beneficiary | **No CGT event** at death (cost base transfers) |
| Beneficiary sells property | CGT on gain above deceased's cost base. Main residence exemption applies if beneficiary sells within 2 years. |
| Portfolio returned to estate | **No CGT event** at death (if unrealised gains) |
| Portfolio liquidated to settle estate | CGT event — gain calculated from deceased's cost base |

### 2.2 Estate Waterfall

```
Property value:              $1,000,000
Less: EPM mortgage balance:  -$720,000 (original $640K + 20 years accrued margin)
Net property equity:          $280,000

Investment portfolio:         $850,000 (grown from $640K over 20 years)
Less: Lender's claim:        -$0 (Model B: lender already owns portfolio)
   OR
Portfolio to estate:          $850,000 (if structured to return to estate)

Other assets:                 $200,000

TOTAL ESTATE:                $480,000 to $1,330,000
(depending on portfolio return structure)
```

### 2.3 Superannuation Interaction

EPM does not affect superannuation death benefit nominations. Super is distributed per binding/non-binding nomination, outside the estate (unless directed to estate). However:

- If consumer drew down super during lifetime because they had EPM income, super balance at death may be lower
- Tax-free vs taxable components of super death benefits follow normal rules
- No interaction between EPM mortgage and super

### 2.4 Centrelink Recovery

Centrelink does **not** recover pension payments from the estate. However, if EPM reduced the consumer's pension during their lifetime (via assets test), that income is permanently lost — it's not "refunded" at death.

**Disclosure:** "If your Age Pension was reduced due to EPM, the foregone pension payments cannot be recovered from your estate or from Centrelink."

## 3. United States — Federal Estate Tax & Step-Up Basis

### 3.1 Estate Tax

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Federal exemption (2026+) | ~$7 million per person (post-TCJA sunset) |
| Portability | Unused exemption transfers to surviving spouse |
| Rate | 40% above exemption |
| Most EPM consumers | **Below threshold** — no federal estate tax |

**State estate taxes** exist in ~17 states with lower thresholds (e.g., Oregon $1M, Massachusetts $2M). These may be relevant for EPM consumers in those states.

### 3.2 Step-Up in Basis

**This is a significant estate benefit in the US.**

| Asset | Cost Basis at Death |
|-------|-------------------|
| Property | Stepped up to FMV at date of death |
| Investment portfolio (if in estate) | Stepped up to FMV at date of death |

**Impact:** If the property appreciated from $800K purchase to $1.2M at death, beneficiaries inherit at $1.2M basis — **no CGT on the appreciation**. Same applies to portfolio holdings.

### 3.3 Estate Waterfall (US)

```
Property FMV at death:       $1,200,000
Less: EPM mortgage balance:  -$720,000
Net property equity:          $480,000 (basis stepped up — no CGT for beneficiary)

Portfolio FMV at death:       $850,000 (basis stepped up)
Other assets:                 $200,000

Gross estate:                $1,530,000
Less: EPM mortgage:          -$720,000 (deductible liability)
Net taxable estate:           $810,000

Federal estate tax:           $0 (below $7M exemption)
```

### 3.4 Medicaid Estate Recovery

**Important:** In most states, Medicaid can seek estate recovery for benefits paid during the consumer's lifetime (long-term care, nursing home). If the consumer received Medicaid benefits, the state may file a claim against the estate.

EPM does not create additional Medicaid exposure, but the property (minus EPM mortgage) may be subject to Medicaid recovery if applicable.

### 3.5 Probate Considerations

- EPM mortgage must be satisfied from estate assets (typically property sale)
- If property is held in a **revocable living trust** (common in US estate planning), EPM mortgage follows the property
- Beneficiaries may choose to refinance the EPM and keep the property (if they qualify for their own mortgage)
- NNEG protects beneficiaries if mortgage exceeds property value

## 4. New Zealand — Clean Transfer, No Death Duties

### 4.1 Estate Settlement

New Zealand has **no estate tax, inheritance tax, or gift duty** (gift duty abolished 2011). This makes NZ the cleanest jurisdiction for EPM estate settlement.

| Event | Tax Treatment |
|-------|---------------|
| Property passes to beneficiary | No tax event |
| Beneficiary sells property | No CGT (NZ has no general capital gains tax) |
| Portfolio returned to estate | No tax event |
| Portfolio liquidated | No CGT |

### 4.2 Estate Waterfall (NZ)

```
Property value:              $1,000,000 NZD
Less: EPM mortgage balance:  -$720,000
Net property equity:          $280,000

Portfolio value:              $850,000 (no FIF tax at death — income ceases)
Other assets:                 $150,000

TOTAL ESTATE:                $1,280,000 (Model B with portfolio return)
                         or  $430,000 (Model B without portfolio return)

Tax on estate:               $0
```

### 4.3 Relationship Property

Under the Property (Relationships) Act 1976:

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Family home | Relationship property — divided equally on separation |
| EPM mortgage | A liability on relationship property |
| Portfolio | If relationship property, divided equally |
| On death | Surviving partner can elect relationship property division OR will entitlements |

**EPM must be structured as joint liability** if the property is relationship property. Both partners must consent to the EPM (similar to standard mortgage requirement).

### 4.4 Family Protection Act

NZ's Family Protection Act 1955 allows family members to challenge a will if they were not adequately provided for. EPM reduces net property equity, which could trigger claims:

**Disclosure:** "If you have dependants or family members who expect to inherit your property, you should discuss the EPM with them and your solicitor. Reduced property equity may affect claims under the Family Protection Act 1955."

## 5. United Kingdom — IHT Planning & NNEG

### 5.1 IHT — The Central Estate Issue

UK is the only EPM region with a significant death tax. IHT at 40% above the nil-rate band makes estate planning the primary concern for UK consumers.

### 5.2 Detailed IHT Modelling

**Scenario: £800,000 property, 80% LTV, 20-year EPM, 7% portfolio growth, 3% lender margin**

| Year | Mortgage Balance | Portfolio Value | Property Value (2% growth) | Net Estate Impact |
|------|-----------------|-----------------|---------------------------|-------------------|
| 0 | £640,000 | £640,000 | £800,000 | £0 (neutral) |
| 5 | £672,000 | £897,000 | £883,000 | +£225,000 |
| 10 | £706,000 | £1,259,000 | £975,000 | +£553,000 |
| 15 | £741,000 | £1,766,000 | £1,076,000 | +£1,025,000 |
| 20 | £778,000 | £2,478,000 | £1,188,000 | +£1,700,000 |

*Note: Consumer has also received ~£600,000 in tax-free income over 20 years, which is NOT in the estate if spent.*

**Wait — the estate INCREASES with EPM?**

In a growth scenario, yes. The portfolio grows faster than the mortgage accrues. But:

1. The consumer received £600K+ tax-free income (the entire point)
2. That income, once spent, is permanently removed from the estate
3. The portfolio growth above the mortgage balance creates **additional estate value** that wouldn't exist without EPM
4. Beneficiaries inherit MORE total value (income consumed + remaining estate) than without EPM

### 5.3 IHT Mitigation Strategies

| Strategy | How It Works with EPM |
|----------|----------------------|
| **Gifts from income (s21 IHTA)** | EPM income qualifies as "normal expenditure out of income" — regular gifts are immediately IHT-exempt |
| **7-year PETs** | Larger gifts from EPM income fall out of estate after 7 years |
| **Charity legacy (s41 IHTA)** | Leave 10%+ of estate to charity → IHT rate drops from 40% to 36% |
| **Loan trust** | EPM income paid into trust — original capital outside estate, growth retained |
| **AIM/BR investments** | Redirect EPM income into Business Relief qualifying investments — IHT-exempt after 2 years |
| **Life insurance in trust** | Use portion of EPM income to fund whole-of-life policy in trust — covers IHT liability |

### 5.4 The RNRB Question

The Residence Nil-Rate Band (£175,000) applies when passing the family home to direct descendants. **Critical question for EPM:**

| Scenario | RNRB Available? |
|----------|----------------|
| Property sold to repay EPM, cash distributed to children | ⚠️ **Possibly not** — RNRB requires passing the residence (or downsizing rules) |
| Beneficiaries inherit property, refinance to repay EPM | ✅ **Yes** — residence passes to direct descendants |
| Property value < EPM mortgage (NNEG applies) | ⚠️ **Uncertain** — no net residence passes, RNRB may not apply |

**Recommendation:** Seek HMRC confirmation on RNRB availability for EPM-encumbered properties. This is worth £70,000 in IHT savings (40% × £175,000).

## 6. The NNEG Guarantee — Beneficiary Protection

### 6.1 What NNEG Means

**No Negative Equity Guarantee:** The consumer (and their estate) will never owe more than the property is worth at the point of sale/settlement.

```
IF mortgage_balance > property_value_at_settlement:
    estate_owes = property_value  (NOT mortgage_balance)
    lender_loss = mortgage_balance - property_value
    beneficiary_liability = $0 beyond property
```

### 6.2 NNEG by Region

| Region | NNEG Status | Authority |
|--------|-------------|-----------|
| **AU** | Recommended (not yet mandated for EPM) | Model on UK ERC standards |
| **US** | Standard for HECM (HUD requirement) | 12 USC §1715z-20(j) — FHA non-recourse |
| **NZ** | Recommended | No specific statute — contractual obligation |
| **UK** | **Mandatory** (ERC membership requirement) | Equity Release Council Standard 1 |

### 6.3 When NNEG Triggers

| Scenario | Property Value | Mortgage Balance | NNEG Triggered? | Estate Owes |
|----------|---------------|-----------------|-----------------|-------------|
| Normal growth | £1,200,000 | £778,000 | No | £778,000 (from property sale) |
| Flat market | £800,000 | £778,000 | No | £778,000 |
| Market decline (-20%) | £640,000 | £778,000 | **Yes** | £640,000 (lender absorbs £138K loss) |
| Market crash (-40%) | £480,000 | £778,000 | **Yes** | £480,000 (lender absorbs £298K loss) |

### 6.4 Beneficiary Communication

At consumer death, beneficiaries receive:

```
Dear [Beneficiary],

[Consumer Name] had a FutureProof Equity Participation Mortgage on [Property Address].

Current mortgage balance:     £778,000
Independent property valuation: £[VALUE]

Your options:
1. Sell the property — EPM mortgage repaid from proceeds, surplus is yours
2. Repay the mortgage — refinance or pay from other assets, keep the property
3. NNEG applies — if the mortgage exceeds the property value, you owe only
   the property value. The difference is absorbed by the lender.

You have [12 months] to make your decision. No interest accrues during
the settlement period beyond the existing mortgage terms.

The investment portfolio [has been / will be] [returned to the estate / 
retained by the lender] per the EPM contract terms.
```

## 8. EPM vs Traditional Equity Release — Estate Comparison

### 8.1 UK Lifetime Mortgage Comparison

| Factor | Traditional Lifetime Mortgage | FutureProof EPM |
|--------|------------------------------|-----------------|
| How income is generated | Loan advances (drawdown from mortgage) | Investment returns on portfolio |
| Interest | Compounds on total drawn amount | Accrues as lender margin only |
| Typical AER | 5-7% MER on drawn balance | ~3% margin on original LTV |
| Estate after 20 years (£640K drawn) | Property less ~£1.7M mortgage = **NNEG likely** | Property less £778K mortgage + £1.2M portfolio = **net positive** |
| NNEG probability | High (compound interest on full balance) | Low (portfolio growth offsets margin) |
| Beneficiary outcome | Little or no inheritance | **Significant inheritance likely** |

### 8.2 Why EPM Is Superior for Estates

Traditional equity release is a **depleting** product — the mortgage balance grows via compound interest on the full amount. After 20 years at 6% AER, a £640,000 drawdown becomes ~£2,050,000 in debt. NNEG almost certainly activates.

EPM is a **growing** product — the mortgage accrues only the lender margin (~3%), while the investment portfolio grows at ~7%. After 20 years:
- Mortgage: ~£778,000 (far less than traditional ER)
- Portfolio: ~£1,200,000+ (additional asset that doesn't exist with traditional ER)

**The portfolio is the estate's safety net.** Even in a poor market, the portfolio provides a buffer that traditional equity release cannot match.
