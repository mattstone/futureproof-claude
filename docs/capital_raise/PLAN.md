## Honest disclosure for VCs (lead with this; share verbatim if asked)

We expect the partner-meeting question: *"why isn't this a SaaS deal?"* Answer it upfront. The following are real reasons EPM is a hard sell for many VC funds. We list them not to talk past them but so a partner can disqualify in 10 minutes if it's not a fit, instead of 6 weeks.

**1. Fund-return math.** EPM economics resemble specialty insurance / asset management more than SaaS. Steady-state EBITDA margin profile is attractive (74% Y3 → 89% Y5 blended on the fee streams; we don't warehouse risk) — but the path to a 10–50× equity outcome depends on platform AUM growth and an eventual strategic / IPO exit, not multiple expansion on ARR. 3-case Y5 revenue spread: $162M / $633M / $2.46B (USD). For SAFE pricing the binding event is Series A 2028 — investors convert at a 30% discount to whatever clears. If your fund needs every position to plausibly return the fund on its own at conversion price, this isn't your deal.

**2. Time to outcome.** A retirement-income platform compounds slowly. Realistic exit window is 7–10 years (super-fund-led acquisition, Challenger-type strategic, or IPO). Doesn't fit a 5-year-from-investment exit assumption.

**3. Capital intensity & funding-stack dependency.** We need wholesale funder partners or warehouse facilities to write volume. We do not warehouse risk on our own balance sheet — but the model requires a credible funding stack and that takes time to build. This is closer to a fintech-marketplace + capital-markets play than a pure software business.

**4. Regulatory surface area.** ASIC (AU); FCA-equivalent regimes if we expand. The Retirement Income Covenant is a tailwind, but it doesn't shorten the licensing or compliance lift.

**5. Mixed comp set.** UK equity-release names (Just Group, Pure Retirement) have had bumpy public histories. Australian comps are sparse. A skeptical analyst will pull up Just's chart and the conversation gets harder. Our differentiator is a fundamentally different product structure — run-off mechanism, no arrears / no redraw mechanics, investment-linked income — but this needs explaining, and a 5-minute pattern-match goes against us.

**6. Market education burden.** Both borrowers and super funds need education. Sales cycle is quarters for super-fund integrations, months for direct-to-consumer. Not a viral / PLG curve.

**7. AI is operating leverage, not a moat we're claiming.** We are AI-native in how we run the business (see next section). We do **not** claim AI gives us a credit-underwriting edge, regulatory shortcut, or investment-return advantage. It lets a small team run a regulated financial services business at software-company overhead — that's the real claim, and we don't stretch it further.

**If any of the above is a hard veto for your fund, please say so on the first call.** We'd rather close the loop fast than burn 4 weeks of process. We're raising from people for whom these tradeoffs are *features* (regulated tailwind, defensible product, durable cashflows) — not bugs.

## The thesis (use this as your pitch frame)

### For FOs
Family office principals are typically 50–75. They've watched a parent run out of liquid retirement savings while sitting on a $2M home. They get this product viscerally in 90 seconds — which generalist VCs never do. **Pitch the human story first, the asset class second, the unit economics third.** Most founders do this in reverse and lose them.

### For VCs
Lead with **market structure, not the human story**. The unlock is the **Retirement Income Covenant** (2022) — a regulated A$3.5T tailwind that legally requires every super fund to have a retirement income strategy. Most still don't have a credible answer. The Callaghan Review (2020) explicitly endorsed home equity release. EPM is the turnkey solution super funds can offer their members. This is a B2B2C distribution play with a defensible product structure (run-off mechanism, no arrears / redraw, investment-linked) and a clear path to platform AUM economics. After market structure, show the **AI-native operating model** (next section) — that's the line that turns the conversation from "interesting financial product" to "venture-scale". *Then* unit economics. *Then* the human story (as proof of demand, not as the lede).

## AI-native operations (the operating-leverage story for VCs)

**The claim, stated narrowly:** A small team (~[N] FTE) runs the end-to-end operations of a regulated financial services business — origination, customer service, marketing, compliance ops, investment management ops — at software-company cost structure, because the platform is built AI-native from day one.

**The claim, stated honestly:** AI does *not* underwrite credit decisions, replace human compliance sign-off, or generate investment alpha. It removes the cost of the work *around* those things — the 70% of FS-company headcount that is process, content, triage, support, and analytics, not regulated decision-making.

### Where AI is genuinely doing work in the platform today
- **Customer-facing agent layer.** Tiered chat agents handle pre-application Q&A, onboarding journeys, and post-issue customer support. Human-in-the-loop escalation for any regulated advice surface. (Implementation: `ChatAgent`, `ChatConversation`, `ChatMessage`, `ai_agent_router.rb` — verify against current code before pitching.)
- **Operations & admin.** Internal admin agents draft summaries, flag exceptions, and prepare review packs for human sign-off. Cuts ops headcount per A$ AUM.
- **Marketing & content.** Multi-jurisdictional content (US/AU/NZ/UK) regenerated and tailored without per-region content teams.
- **Customer service triage.** First-line classification, sentiment, priority routing — humans handle resolution; AI handles routing.
- **Investment management ops.** Portfolio rebalancing alerts, anomaly detection, reporting pack generation. Investment *decisions* remain rules-based / human-reviewed; AI handles the surrounding workflow.

### The unit-economics consequence (this is what VCs care about)
Operating cost per A$ AUM at scale [insert latest model output]. The relevant comp is not "another mortgage lender" — it's "what would this business look like if we were a 200-person specialty insurer? We're not, because the AI ops layer absorbs that headcount."

### What we don't claim (state this explicitly in pitches)
- AI does not generate credit-underwriting edge — that's Futureproof's proprietary financial model, deterministic math.
- AI does not shorten ASIC / FCA licensing timelines.
- AI does not replace the wholesale funder / warehouse facility need.
- AI does not produce investment alpha — investment policy is rules-based, transparent, and auditable.

This is the realistic AI story. It will hold up to a sharp partner asking *"so what does the AI actually do?"* — which is the question that kills 80% of AI-pitched deals in 2026.

**Goal: lock one early-conviction SAFE at ≥ USD $1M (the round minimum).** Early-conviction investor can come from any track. Their cheque captures the highest discount tier and signals to followers — without it, the rolling close stalls.

## Honest tradeoffs (read these before starting)

1. **Family-office-led rounds are slow.** 6 months, not 3. Burn rate planning assumes this.
2. **USD $1M is the SAFE minimum** — keeps the SAFE pool clean and the cap-table-at-conversion manageable. No nominee/SPV needed for sub-cap cheques because there are none.
3. **You may end up with passive money.** FOs rarely add operational value the way VCs claim to. Series A still needs an institutional lead — don't burn VC bridges. **This is the #1 reason for the dual-track:** a fintech-specialist VC on the cap table at this round materially improves Series A optionality.
4. **Discretion is currency for FOs.** Don't name FO investors publicly without explicit permission.
5. **VCs and SAFE rounds.** VCs accustomed to leading priced rounds will sometimes push for terms outside the SAFE template (governance seats, observer rights, special economics). The standard answer is no — the round is a SAFE on standard terms; bespoke economics break MFN. A VC who insists is signalling a Series A conversation, not a SAFE conversation.
6. **A "no" is rarely real.** Quarterly update list — ~30% of FOs and ~20% of VCs come back next round.
